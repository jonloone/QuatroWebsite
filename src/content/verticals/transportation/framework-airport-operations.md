## Section 2 — PROBLEM DEPTH (Quantified, Not Just Named)

> **Framework rule:** The sub-page SIZES the problem. Attach specific metrics. Give the buyer ammunition for internal justification.

**Label:** `THE AIRPORT COORDINATION CRISIS`

### The Operational Reality

Commercial aircraft operate on tight turnaround schedules — 30 minutes from wheels down to pushback for the next flight. Each arrival requires runway coordination, gate assignment, baggage discharge, aircraft servicing, cabin cleaning, loading, security checks, and passenger boarding. Each departure requires runway clearance, ground equipment movement, fuel service, and air traffic control coordination. Every step is simultaneous, every step is dependent on manual communication between runway controllers, gate agents, baggage handlers, maintenance teams, catering, security, and airlines.

### The Cost of the Status Quo

Your airport operates in information silos. Air traffic controllers know runway status. Gate agents know passenger arrival. Baggage handlers know discharge timing. Maintenance teams know aircraft servicability status. Security checkpoints know passenger flow. Catering knows loading readiness. Each sees their domain. None sees the coordinated 30-minute turnaround cycle.

| Coordination Gap | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Runway availability not predicted with gate readiness | Aircraft arrives; gate is still occupied by previous flight; aircraft waits on taxiway for 15–30 minutes |
| Baggage discharge timing not coordinated with ground equipment | Aircraft is ready to discharge; baggage system is serving another aircraft; discharge delayed; turnaround extends 20+ minutes |
| Aircraft maintenance status not predicted during approach | Aircraft lands; maintenance discovers mechanical issue during post-flight; aircraft removed from service; cascading delays across the day |
| Passenger connection timing not optimized | Aircraft lands; baggage takes 20 minutes to discharge; connecting passengers miss final boarding; flights depart late or passengers rebooked |
| Security incidents not detected until checkpoint | A security threat only detected when passengers reach the terminal; evacuation required; airport operations disrupted for hours |

### Why It Hasn't Been Solved

The adversary (disruption, whether intentional or accidental) has precise timing windows — a single system failure cascades across the entire airport. Your teams see separate systems. This asymmetry is not a failure of equipment or people. It is a failure of operational architecture — the airport is designed around sequential coordination, not real-time orchestration. And it is the gap that every modern airport — especially those managing growth in LATAM expansion — must close before operational efficiency becomes possible.

*Framework note: The coordination crisis is the quantified pain — framed as a structural airport architecture problem rather than an effort problem. The five-row table gives the buyer specific ammunition: "here's what we lose because our systems don't coordinate." The reference to security incidents and cascading delays signals critical infrastructure relevance.*

## Section 3 — HOW IT WORKS (Operational, Not Architectural)

> **Framework rule:** Show what changes day-to-day. Name the source systems. Describe the scene the buyer can picture.

**Four Operational Domains — Palantir Vertical List**

Each domain produces intelligence independently. All connect through a single fused runway-to-gate coordination picture. An aircraft arrival in one domain automatically triggers preparations across all others.

### Runway & Airfield Management
**What connects:** Air traffic control radar, ground radar, runway sensor systems (inductive loops, pressure sensors), parking position databases, weather observation systems.
**What the team sees:** Aircraft approach predictions accurate to the minute. Runway and taxiway occupancy in real-time. Gate availability predicted 15 minutes before landing. Ground equipment staging locations calculated for efficient aircraft movement. Weather-driven capacity degradation alerts (wind, precipitation) trigger pre-emptive schedule adjustments.
**What outcome it drives:** On-time performance increases 8–15%. Gate delays eliminated. Runway efficiency optimized.

### Passenger Flow & Terminal Operations
**What connects:** Passenger information systems, boarding gate displays, security checkpoint sensors, retail/lounge occupancy monitoring, terminal camera systems.
**What the team sees:** Passenger arrival flow predicted from aircraft approach. Gate occupancy flow managed through terminal visualization. Security checkpoint queues predicted and staffed proactively. Connection times optimized — connecting passengers routed efficiently through terminal. Passenger behavior anomalies detected (unauthorized movement, extended loitering) flagged for security.
**What outcome it drives:** Passenger experience improves significantly. Connection success rates increase 5–10%. Security incidents detected early.

### Baggage & Ground Support Systems
**What connects:** Baggage handling system (BHS) status, ground support equipment (GSE) position and maintenance status, aircraft cargo management systems, loading/unloading operations monitoring.
**What the team sees:** Baggage discharge timing predicted with aircraft approach. BHS equipment health monitored continuously — conveyor failures, sorter jams detected before discharge window. GSE staging optimized — tugs, loaders, cargo containers pre-positioned. Aircraft weight and balance calculated in real-time to ensure efficient loading.
**What outcome it drives:** Baggage handling delays reduced 20–30%. Equipment failures don't extend turnaround. Cargo operations seamless.

### Perimeter Security & Critical Infrastructure
**What connects:** Perimeter camera networks (CCTV), intrusion detection sensors, access control systems, security checkpoint analytics, threat assessment databases.
**What the team sees:** Perimeter activity monitored 24/7 — unauthorized access attempts detected sub-minute. Security checkpoint queues and throughput optimized for efficiency. Passenger behavior anomalies correlated with security risk databases. Aircraft approach patterns monitored for anomalous activity (unscheduled arrivals, unusual approach vectors). Integration with border/security agencies for flight manifest risk assessment.
**What outcome it drives:** Security posture maintained continuously. Perimeter breaches detected and responded to within minutes. Border/security coordination integrated into airport operations without operational disruption.

## Section 5 — OBJECTION HANDLING (Woven Into Narrative)

> **Framework rule:** 2-3 objections woven as confident statements, not FAQ.

**Objection 1: "Our airport systems are fragmented and old — integration is impossible"**
> The utility connects to what you have today — ATC systems, baggage handling systems, passenger information displays, access control systems. Integration happens at the data layer, not the system layer. No rip-and-replace. Sensor gaps (terminal occupancy cameras, GSE position tracking) are identified during the pilot and filled incrementally. You start operations with what exists.

**Objection 2: "We have real-time baggage systems already — we don't need another monitoring tool"**
> Baggage system health is only one piece. The value comes from coordinating baggage timing with runway availability, passenger flow, and security. When your BHS tells you a carousel is ready and your runway says the aircraft is 5 minutes from arrival and your passenger flow shows connecting passengers are queued at security — that's when you have operational intelligence. Single-system monitoring is data. Cross-system correlation is intelligence.

**Objection 3: "Security and operations can't share data — they're separate for a reason"**
> Role-based access controls ensure security teams see only security-relevant intelligence — manifest risk scores, perimeter anomalies, threat assessments. They never see passenger names, destination data, or commercial flight information. Flight manifest data shared for risk assessment only. Every data access is logged and auditable. The utility handles data governance; security and operations focus on their missions.

*Framework note: Three objections mapped to the airport buyer's specific concerns — aging systems, redundant monitoring, and inter-agency data sharing. Each resolved in 2-3 confident sentences. No "FAQ" label.*
