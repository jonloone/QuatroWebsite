## Section 2 — PROBLEM DEPTH (Quantified, Not Just Named)

> **Framework rule:** SIZE the problem. Dollar figure, time cost, or error rate. Give the buyer ammunition.

**Label:** `THE INVISIBLE DRAIN`

### The Operational Reality

Water utilities in Latin America lose 40-50% of treated water to leaks in distribution networks. This isn't theoretical. In major cities, the figure exceeds 50%. A typical mid-sized city system treating 200,000 m³/day loses 100,000 m³/day to undetected leaks. At regional water cost of $0.60/m³, that's $60,000/day or $21.9M/year in lost treated water.

But the water loss is only the visible cost. Behind it: revenue loss from unmeasured consumption, operational inefficiency from out-of-tolerance pressure zones, environmental risk from pipe degradation enabling contamination ingress, and regulatory exposure when quality violations occur.

### The Cost of the Status Quo

| Gap | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Detection latency | A leak starts. The operator doesn't know it exists until water pressure drops system-wide or customers complain. By then, 100,000s of cubic meters are already lost. |
| Manual leak localization | Finding the leak requires field crews with acoustic sensors and system knowledge. Average locate time: 5–14 days. During that window, water loss continues. |
| No predictive health | Pipes age silently until catastrophic failure. The operator knows a pipe is failing when it breaks and disrupts service. Average pipe replacement cost: $5,000–$20,000 per 100m. |
| Compliance blind spots | SENAGUA requires real-time water quality and system pressure reporting. Current practice: manual data collection, monthly aggregation, lagged reporting. Compliance violations discovered through audits. |
| Revenue leakage | Undetected water loss = unmeasured consumption. Billing systems show treated volume but can't account for where it went. Revenue reconciliation happens in arrears. |

### Why It Hasn't Been Solved

Water operators have built systems incrementally over decades. A SCADA system for treatment. A separate meter network. Separate pressure sensors at critical points. A manual reporting system for regulators. These systems don't talk to each other. The operator manages them through shift logs and spreadsheets.

What's needed is hydraulic correlation — the ability to correlate pressure changes across the entire network simultaneously, identify where a leak must be occurring mathematically, and alert operators with actionable intelligence in real-time, not days later.

*Framework note: The cost table gives the buyer five specific ammunition points. The 40-50% water loss figure (quantified as $21.9M/year) frames water system operations as a revenue problem, not just an operational one — widening the buyer base to finance and executive leadership. The hydraulic correlation concept (pressure correlation) is the technical hook that differentiates this from simple leak detection.*

## Section 3 — HOW IT WORKS (Operational, Not Architectural)

> **Framework rule:** Show what changes day-to-day. Name source systems. Describe the scene. Each capability: what connects → what team sees → outcome.

**Six Operational Capabilities — Card Grid**

### 01 — Hydraulic Network Correlation
**What connects:** Real-time pressure sensors at every major junction, flow meters on every distribution main, and SCADA data from treatment plants feed into a unified hydraulic model that mirrors the actual network topology.

**What the team sees:** The operator opens the dashboard at shift change. The system shows actual vs. modeled pressure at every major junction in real-time. Any deviation signals a leak. The system automatically correlates pressure changes across multiple points to isolate the leak's probable location — not just "there's a leak somewhere," but "the leak is in main line 47-B between junctions 12 and 15."

**What outcome it drives:** Leaks detected within minutes of occurrence, not days. Localization accuracy enabling surgical repairs instead of system-wide searches.

**Named integration:** SCADA systems (Wonderware, Ignition, etc.), pressure sensor networks (analog, digital, IoT), flow meters (any protocol), GIS systems for network topology.

### 02 — Automated Leak Alert & Dispatch
**What connects:** Real-time pressure deviation triggers automated alert workflow. Alert includes location probability map, segment characteristics (age, material, installation date), repair crew availability, and material recommendations.

**What the team sees:** An alert arrives on the operator's dashboard and mobile device: "Likely leak on main line 47-B, segment installed 1998, probable loss 50–150 L/min, 2 crews available in proximity." The operator confirms dispatch with one click. The system logs the alert time, detected location, and actions taken.

**What outcome it drives:** Repair crews mobilize within hours, not days. Lost water is minimized. Regulatory chain-of-custody documented from detection to resolution.

### 03 — Pressure Zone Optimization
**What connects:** Continuous pressure monitoring across all zones reveals where pressure exceeds regulated max (100m head) or falls below service minimum (20m head).

**What the team sees:** Dashboard shows green (compliant), yellow (approaching limits), red (out of spec) for each zone. Historical pressure profiles reveal patterns — peak demand hours, overnight pressure creep, seasonal variation. The system recommends zone adjustment and rehab sequence to optimize pressure zones globally.

**What outcome it drives:** Reduced burst frequency (each 1m head reduction = 0.8% fewer bursts). Improved service reliability. Better water quality (lower pressure zones reduce contamination ingress through micro-breaks).

### 04 — Predictive Pipe Health & Failure Risk
**What connects:** Historical water quality data, pressure history, pipe material and installation date metadata, maintenance logs, and customer complaint data feed into models that predict which segments are aging fastest.

**What the team sees:** Asset heat map showing pipe failure risk by segment. Green = newly installed or recently replaced. Yellow = approaching age-based replacement window. Red = high failure probability within 12 months. The system recommends replacement sequence optimized for cost and risk reduction.

**What outcome it drives:** Prioritized replacement scheduling. Prevention of catastrophic failures. Reduced emergency repairs. Optimized capex spend on pipe rehabilitation.

### 05 — Water Quality Monitoring Integration
**What connects:** Water quality monitors (turbidity, pH, residual chlorine, pressure, temperature) at multiple points in the distribution network feed into quality anomaly detection.

**What the team sees:** Quality dashboard showing real-time compliance against SENAGUA thresholds at each monitoring point. Any out-of-spec event is flagged with probable cause: "Low residual chlorine at junction 8 — likely due to detention time > 24 hrs in line 47. Recommend increased chlorine dosing or flow rerouting."

**What outcome it drives:** Real-time quality assurance. Compliance violations prevented before they reach customers. Evidence trail for regulatory reporting.

### 06 — Automated SENAGUA Compliance Reporting
**What connects:** All operational data (pressure, flow, quality, incidents, maintenance, repairs) feeds into compliance reporting templates that match SENAGUA regulatory requirements.

**What the team sees:** Compliance dashboard. System shows current month's reporting status — green (complete, submitted), yellow (pending final data), red (incomplete). No spreadsheets. No manual assembly. The system auto-generates monthly and quarterly reports tied to actual operational data and submits via SENAGUA portal if authorized.

**What outcome it drives:** Guaranteed on-time compliance. No audit findings for missing documentation. Regulatory confidence. Reduced compliance burden on operations teams.

*Framework note: Six capabilities, each structured per framework. Named integration partners (SCADA vendors, sensor types) provide third-party credibility. SENAGUA is the buyer's own institutional context — they recognize it immediately. The progression from detection (01-02) through optimization (03-05) to compliance (06) mirrors the operational workflow.*

## Section 5 — OBJECTION HANDLING (Woven Into Narrative)

> **Framework rule:** 2-3 objections woven as confident statements, not FAQ.

**Objection 1: "Leak detection is slow and still requires field crews to physically locate the source"**
> Real-time hydraulic correlation pinpoints leaks to specific segments within minutes of occurrence. Our models predict leak location from pressure signatures alone — not just "there's a leak," but "the leak is in main line 47-B between junctions 12 and 15." Field crews arrive with a target location, not a search area. Average locate time: 2–4 hours instead of 5–14 days.

**Objection 2: "We already have SCADA. Why do we need another system?"**
> Quatro connects to your existing SCADA — no replacement required. We integrate your existing pressure sensors, flow meters, and treatment plant data, add real-time hydraulic modeling and leak detection that SCADA wasn't designed to do, and deliver operational intelligence your team can act on. SCADA is a control system. Quatro is an intelligence system that sits alongside it, extracting actionable insights from the data your infrastructure already generates.

**Objection 3: "Compliance reporting is already automated in our municipal finance system"**
> Most municipal systems aggregate historical data monthly or quarterly, with significant lag. SENAGUA increasingly requires real-time reporting and immediate notification of quality or system events. Quatro delivers real-time compliance verification — operators know immediately when they're out of spec, can respond in real-time, and have full documentation of the event and response for regulatory audit. Compliance becomes operational, not administrative.

*Framework note: Three objections — detection latency, existing SCADA integration, compliance reporting — mapped to the water utility buyer's most likely concerns. The SCADA objection ("sits alongside, doesn't replace") is particularly powerful because it removes the "rip and replace" barrier to adoption.*
