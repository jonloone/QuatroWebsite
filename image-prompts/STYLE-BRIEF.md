# Quatro Image Generation — Style Brief v7.1

**Style updated:** April 2026
**v7.1 additions:** Six defined shot types (World, Systems, People, Detail, Vantage, Transition). IBM 2x grid composition formally adopted. Optimism mandate — light, bright, forward-looking imagery across all verticals. Expanded per-vertical light and shot type guidance.
**Key shift from v6:** From isolated macro object portraits → **cinematic editorial photography of industry worlds**. Pull back. Show the environment, the infrastructure ecosystem, the broader landscape. Each image should feel like a frame from a documentary — you're inside the world of that industry, surrounded by its reality. Warm, human, grounded.

**Reference:** IBM Design Language photography (documentary, editorial, authentic) — but warmer. IBM avoids golden hour; we embrace it. IBM is cool and clinical; we're warm analog film. Think IBM's composition discipline + Samsara's warmth and reverence.

---

## Creative Philosophy

Every Quatro image is a **cinematic frame from inside an industry's world**. Not a zoomed-in texture study. Not a stock photo of a facility. A moment captured by a documentary filmmaker who is embedded in that industry — standing on the factory floor, riding the rail line, walking through the ground station at dawn.

The viewer should feel **immersed in a real environment** that represents the full lifecycle of that industry. They should feel the scale, the systems, the orchestration. The image tells a story: this is what this industry looks like when it's running.

**The sweet spot:** You're close enough to feel the environment's character (the light, the surfaces, the atmosphere) but pulled back enough to understand the SYSTEM. You see infrastructure in context — not a single bolt, but the machine it belongs to. Not a single fiber, but the cable tray full of hundreds.

**Reference points:**
- **IBM Design Language** — editorial authenticity, 2x grid composition, step-back perspective that shows context and environment. Their principle of being an "objective observer of the working world"
- **Ron Fricke (Samsara/Koyaanisqatsi)** — systems as poetry. Infrastructure as meditation. The mundane made transcendent through beautiful cinematography
- **Christopher Payne** — manufacturing process as visual drama. The beauty of environments where things are made
- **Edward Burtynsky** — industrial landscapes that reveal pattern, scale, and the sublime in infrastructure

But WARMER than all of them. Quatro's palette leans into golden hour, amber practical light, and analog film warmth.

## The Film Look

Cinematic documentary, graded like Kodak Portra 800:

- **35mm or 50mm lens** — environmental, immersive, natural perspective (NOT 85mm macro anymore)
- **f/2.8–f/4 aperture** — enough depth of field to show environment context, but still with pleasing bokeh on distant elements
- **Kodak Portra 800 character** — warm undertones, fine organic grain, rich shadow detail, slightly muted highlights
- **Green-shifted shadows** — all shadow areas push toward forest green (#0f4c3a)
- **Warm amber highlights** — practical light sources carry golden warmth (#F4A261)
- **Visible film grain** — organic, fine, like a high-res 35mm scan
- **Natural vignetting** — subtle, drawing eye to the compositional anchor

**What this is NOT:**
- Not extreme macro / object portraits (that was v6)
- Not compressed, dithered, or halftoned (the site's Bayer shader handles that)
- Not stock photography — no generic, no staged, no clipart quality
- Not illustration, CGI, or digital art
- Not oversaturated or HDR
- Not overly abstract — the viewer should understand what industry they're looking at

## Composition: IBM's Grid Discipline + Warm Optimism

We borrow IBM's structural discipline — the 2x grid, divisions of two, clear focal anchors — but we reject their neutral, clinical mood. Quatro images are **warm, optimistic, and forward-looking**. They show infrastructure that works, systems that hum, communities that benefit. The viewer should feel *good* about the world they're seeing.

### The 2x Grid

Every image divides into two zones — horizontally or vertically:

- **Subject zone:** The primary infrastructure, environment, or activity
- **Context zone:** The broader world that gives the subject meaning — sky, landscape, surrounding systems, or operational space

The boundary between zones is where visual energy lives. A horizon line, an architectural edge, a natural transition from ground to sky. This is IBM's most useful principle: it prevents cluttered compositions while keeping images rich with context.

### Focal Anchor

Every image has **one** primary element the eye goes to. It can be a light source (golden hour sun through equipment), a convergence point (lines of infrastructure meeting), or a human figure showing scale. The anchor sits on or near a grid intersection — never dead center, never fighting with a second anchor.

### Perspective: Two Primary Viewpoints

**Eye-level (primary — 70% of images):**
The documentary filmmaker standing in the space. This is Quatro's dominant perspective — immersive, equal, authentic. You're inside the industry, experiencing it at human scale. Good for heroes, capability visuals, and editorial sections. Always take a step back — show enough environment that the viewer understands the world, not just the object.

**Elevated / aerial (secondary — 30% of images):**
The operations view. Looking down at scale, pattern, and orchestration. Good for fleet networks, infrastructure grids, campus/facility overviews, and "systems working together" narratives. Keep it at 20-45 degrees rather than straight overhead — maintain a sense of dimension and atmosphere.

### Sharp Focus Throughout

f/2.8–f/4 depth of field. Unlike v6's shallow macro blur, everything should be in sharp focus. IBM's principle here is right: sharp focus signals deep interest in the entire environment, not just the hero object. Background should be readable, not blown out. Context matters.

### Composition Don'ts (adapted from IBM)

- No empty centers — every image has a center of interest
- No conflicting focal points — one anchor, one story
- No forced or Dutch angles — either eye-level or aerial, nothing in between
- No artificially manufactured scenes — documentary authenticity
- No flat or dimensionless framing — light and depth create the third dimension

## Shot Types: The Quatro Visual Vocabulary

Adapted from IBM's three photographic categories (Reportage, Portraiture, Still-life) into six shot types tailored to Quatro's needs. Every image you generate should be identifiable as one of these types.

### 1. The World Shot (IBM: Reportage — Working World)

**What it is:** The defining image of an industry. A wide, immersive frame that puts the viewer inside the complete ecosystem — the infrastructure, the environment, the atmosphere, the scale. This is the documentary filmmaker's establishing shot, but at eye level rather than from a helicopter.

**Composition:** Horizontal divisions of two. Infrastructure in the lower zone, sky/atmosphere in the upper. Or vertical: dense operational area on one side, open environment on the other. Always a clear horizon or architectural boundary dividing the zones.

**Light:** This is where Quatro's optimism lives. **Golden hour, blue hour, or clear bright daylight** — never overcast gray, never midnight darkness. The light itself tells a story of possibility: dawn over a ground station (new day, new signal), late afternoon sun warming a smart city intersection (the city working well), clear morning light on a water treatment facility (transparency, health).

**Mood:** Expansive, optimistic, awe-inspiring. The viewer should feel "this world is impressive and it works." Not dark, not dystopian, not gritty.

**Use for:** Hero images (1920×1080), homepage sections, page openers.

**Examples:**
- Ground station array at golden hour, dishes silhouetted against amber sky, the upper half all warm atmosphere
- Solar farm at dawn, rows of panels catching first light, mist burning off in the background
- Port facility in clear late-afternoon sun, cranes and containers in ordered arrangement, blue sky above

### 2. The Systems Shot (IBM: Reportage — Working World, tighter)

**What it is:** A medium-wide frame that shows a **specific subsystem or operational cluster** within the broader world. Tighter than the World Shot but still environmental — you see the system in context, not isolated. Think "one department of the orchestra" rather than "one instrument."

**Composition:** The subject system fills 40-60% of the frame. Surrounding context provides orientation. The 2x grid division might be foreground system / background environment, or detailed equipment / open operational space beside it.

**Light:** Practical light sources mixed with natural ambient. LED arrays in a facility, screen glow in a control room, sunlight streaming through high windows onto equipment. **Always some natural light present** — even in interior shots, windows or skylights bring the outside world in. This keeps the mood bright and connected, never bunker-like.

**Mood:** Capable, organized, humming. The feeling of systems working as designed. Operators who know what they're doing. Technology that serves its environment.

**Use for:** Capability visuals (800×600), solution detail sections, featured capabilities.

**Examples:**
- Battery storage array in a bright facility, sunlight from high windows, status LEDs glowing green
- Network operations center with daylight through glass walls, screens showing healthy dashboards
- Water treatment control room, morning light reflecting off settling tanks visible through the window

### 3. The People Shot (IBM: Portraiture — Progress Partners)

**What it is:** Human presence that shows **scale, expertise, and purpose** — but never identifiable faces. People appear as silhouettes against bright backgrounds, figures in motion, hands on controls, or viewed from behind looking at their operational environment. The human is always in relationship to the system — showing partnership, not domination.

**Composition:** The human figure anchors one zone of the 2x grid. The system or environment occupies the other. The person is always smaller than the infrastructure — showing scale and the scope of what they operate. Light typically wraps around or backlights the figure, creating a warm silhouette.

**Light:** **Strong backlighting or side lighting** — this creates the silhouette/anonymity naturally while producing the most optimistic, warm, aspirational mood. Figure against bright sky, figure in doorway of a sun-lit facility, operator silhouetted against bank of screens.

**Mood:** Aspirational, capable, purposeful. The human element makes infrastructure feel alive and intentional. These images answer: "who operates these systems, and why does it matter?"

**Use for:** Editorial sections, about/mission pages, partnership content, any context where human agency matters.

**Examples:**
- Operator silhouetted in doorway of a bright ground station, sunrise filling the frame behind them
- Engineer viewed from behind, looking out over a sunlit infrastructure campus
- Two technicians in conversation (no faces), hard hats, with wind turbines in clear blue sky behind them

### 4. The Detail Shot (IBM: Still-life — Exhibiting Excellence)

**What it is:** A closer look at a specific element, interface, or artifact that reveals **craft, quality, and intentional design**. Not the extreme macro of v6 — you still see enough context to understand where the object lives. Think "the well-maintained instrument panel" rather than "abstract closeup of a bolt."

**Composition:** The detail fills 50-70% of frame. Surrounding environment provides context at reduced detail (via natural depth of field at f/2.8-4). Clear focal anchor on the primary detail element. Background should suggest the environment without competing.

**Light:** The most controlled lighting of any shot type. Directional light that reveals texture, material quality, and design intent. Warm light on metal and glass surfaces. Status indicators (LEDs, screens) providing accent color. **Always bright enough to read detail** — no murky shadows hiding the subject.

**Mood:** Precision, reliability, craft. The beauty of well-designed systems up close. The viewer should feel confidence in the quality of what they're seeing.

**Use for:** Challenge icons (400×400), inline editorial images, product/capability detail, supporting visuals.

**Examples:**
- Fiber optic patch panel, warm facility light revealing the organized color-coded cables, rack context visible
- SCADA display showing healthy system status, bright screen against a daylit control room
- IoT sensor cluster on a pole, clear blue sky, sunlight catching the metallic housing

### 5. The Vantage Shot (IBM: Reportage — elevated/aerial variant)

**What it is:** The elevated perspective that reveals **pattern, network, and orchestration**. Looking down at 20-45 degrees on infrastructure systems arranged in space. This shot type answers: "how does it all connect?" It reveals the operational intelligence that's invisible from ground level.

**Composition:** The entire frame is the subject — a field of infrastructure, a campus, a fleet in motion, a network of connected systems. The 2x grid division might be built environment / natural environment, or dense operational area / transitional space. Pattern and repetition create visual rhythm.

**Light:** **Clear daylight or golden hour** — the elevated perspective needs broad, even light to reveal the pattern. Harsh midday sun actually works well for overhead/elevated shots because it eliminates shadows and shows detail. Late afternoon creates long shadows that reveal three-dimensional form and add warm tones.

**Mood:** Orchestrated, connected, impressive in scale. The viewer sees the system that's invisible from the ground. This should feel revelatory and optimistic — "look how beautifully this all fits together."

**Use for:** Hero alternatives, "how it works" sections, network/fleet content, homepage capability showcases.

**Examples:**
- Solar farm from elevated angle, geometric rows catching afternoon light, green landscape surrounding
- Port facility from hillside vantage, containers in ordered grid, cranes at work, harbor in clear light
- Smart city intersection from elevated angle, sensor infrastructure visible, afternoon shadows showing form

### 6. The Transition Shot (Quatro-specific)

**What it is:** A frame that captures the **boundary between two states** — physical and digital, old and new, challenge and solution, nature and infrastructure. This is uniquely Quatro — showing the integration point where intelligence meets the physical world.

**Composition:** The frame is divided by a natural transition line: a doorway, a window, a horizon, a screen edge, an architectural boundary. One side shows one state, the other shows the next. The viewer's eye crosses the boundary and experiences the contrast.

**Light:** Different light on each side of the transition — warm practical light in the operational space, cooler ambient or screen glow in the digital space. Or natural light outside, engineered light inside. The light difference reinforces the two-state narrative.

**Mood:** Transformational, bridging, forward-moving. This is the "before/after" or "physical/digital" in a single frame. It should feel like crossing a threshold into something better.

**Use for:** Section transitions, integration messaging, "connect the physical and digital" content, homepage narrative flow.

**Examples:**
- View through a control room window: bright outdoor infrastructure visible through glass, screens and controls in the foreground
- Doorway of a modern facility: warm sunlit operations space visible through the frame, darker hallway in foreground creating natural transition
- Old analog gauge panel on left, modern digital dashboard on right, natural boundary between them

## Mood & Light: The Optimism Mandate

**This is where Quatro diverges most from IBM.** IBM's photography is deliberately neutral — an "objective observer." Quatro's photography is **warmly optimistic**. We are not a detached observer; we are showing what's possible when systems work as intended.

### Light Hierarchy (from most to least preferred)

1. **Golden hour / blue hour** — the magic hours. Warm, dimensional, inherently optimistic. Dawn suggests beginning; dusk suggests completion. This is Quatro's signature light
2. **Clear bright daylight** — honest, transparent, nothing hidden. Works especially well for elevated/vantage shots and infrastructure in natural settings
3. **Warm interior + natural ambient** — facilities with windows, control rooms with daylight mixing in. Never sealed bunkers
4. **Bright overcast** — soft, even light for detail shots. Not gloomy gray — luminous white sky
5. **Night with warm practical light** — operations centers, but only when equipment glow and warm task lighting dominate (not cold fluorescent)

### What to Avoid in Light & Mood

- **Pure darkness / night exteriors** — unless the subject itself is luminous (screen walls, LED arrays)
- **Cold fluorescent / institutional lighting** — makes everything feel clinical and inhuman
- **Overcast gray without warmth** — reads as gloomy and industrial
- **Underground / bunker / windowless** — loses the connection to the outside world and optimism
- **Harsh contrast with lost shadows** — if you can't see detail in the dark areas, it's too dark
- **Rain, fog, or degraded weather** — save this for Challenge images only

## The Industry Landscape Approach

Each industry hero should feel like you're INSIDE that industry's world. The image captures the broader ecosystem — not one piece of hardware, but the environment where that hardware lives and operates.

**The test:** Can someone in that industry look at the hero and say "yes, that's my world"? Not "that's a picture of a bolt from my world." The whole world.

### Energy
- **The world:** Containerized battery storage facilities in clear daylight. Smart grid control centers with wall-to-wall displays and windows. Offshore wind farms from service vessel perspective in bright conditions. EV supercharger hubs at golden hour. Hydrogen electrolysis interiors with high ceilings. HVDC converter stations. The infrastructure of energy in 2026 is distributed, dense, and sensored — not pastoral power lines at sunset
- **Mood:** Vast, purposeful, forward-looking. The physical layer of renewable integration and grid intelligence. Clean energy should feel clean — bright, open, optimistic
- **Light:** Clear afternoon sun on outdoor facilities, golden hour warmth on metal surfaces. Daylit control rooms with screen accents. Dawn over water-adjacent infrastructure — mist burning off. Safety amber from practical lighting as accent, not dominant
- **Shot types:** World Shots for solar/wind farms in landscape. Systems Shots for battery arrays and control rooms. Vantage Shots for infrastructure campuses from above. Detail Shots for sensor clusters and interface panels

### Networks & Telecommunications
- **The world:** Rooftop 5G small cell arrays at golden hour against city skyline. Submarine cable landing stations in coastal daylight. Network operations centers with natural light through glass walls. Fiber splicing trucks on sunlit rural roads. Internet exchange points with dense cross-connect panels. Edge compute enclosures. The invisible distributed infrastructure that makes connectivity real — not a single heroic tower but dozens of interconnected nodes
- **Mood:** Precise but warm. The hidden infrastructure of connectivity made visible and celebrated. These systems connect communities — the mood should reflect that human purpose
- **Light:** Golden hour on outdoor equipment. Daylit NOCs with screen accents (not dark rooms). Twilight sky behind antenna silhouettes. Warm overhead mixing with teal LED status indicators
- **Shot types:** World Shots for rooftop arrays against skyline. Systems Shots for NOCs and exchange points. Detail Shots for cross-connect panels and fiber. Vantage Shots for campus-scale infrastructure

### Smart Cities
- **The world:** IoT sensor poles carrying multiple instrumentation packages on urban intersections in clear daylight. Smart traffic operations centers with city views through glass. Digital twin visualization rooms. Active urban corridors with visible smart infrastructure. Waste management systems in bright morning light. The sensor layer woven into everyday infrastructure — visible when you know where to look
- **Mood:** Urban vitality. The city's nervous system made visible through thoughtful instrumentation that improves daily life. Not surveillance — service. Not monitoring — enabling
- **Light:** Clear afternoon daylight showing sensor hardware detail. Warm interior light in operations centers with city views. Golden hour on urban streetscapes with visible smart infrastructure. Emerald status accents
- **Shot types:** World Shots for instrumented intersections and cityscapes. People Shots for operators overlooking city views. Vantage Shots for smart districts from above. Detail Shots for sensor clusters

### Defense & Intelligence
- **The world:** ISR drone on a tarmac at dawn with warm horizon. Satellite ground station arrays in open desert under big sky. Operations centers with large displays — glass-walled, not bunker-like. Radar installations against dramatic sky. Perimeter infrastructure at golden hour. Communication antenna farms at sunrise
- **Mood:** Deliberate, clear-eyed, sovereign. These are systems that protect — the mood should convey confidence and vigilance, not darkness. Even defense imagery benefits from warmth: dawn patrols, clear-sky operations, the quiet competence of well-maintained systems
- **Light:** Dawn and dusk — the operational hours. Warm horizon light on outdoor installations. Daylit operations centers (glass walls, natural light mixing with screen glow). Clear sky behind radar silhouettes. Amber from practical/safety lighting. We no longer suppress warmth here — defense systems operating in daylight are more optimistic and more honest than the "dark room" cliché
- **Shot types:** World Shots for ground stations in landscape. Systems Shots for operations centers with natural light. Vantage Shots for perimeter infrastructure layouts. Transition Shots for the boundary between secured and unsecured space

### Industrial & Manufacturing
- **The world:** Collaborative manufacturing cells where humans and cobots work side by side, lit by high factory windows. Digital twin dashboard rooms showing real-time factory state. Metal 3D printing bays. AGV fleets navigating bright warehouse corridors. Predictive maintenance sensor arrays on critical equipment. The factory of 2026 is integrated, instrumented, and bathed in good light — high ceilings, skylights, clerestory windows
- **Mood:** Human-machine partnership in well-designed spaces. Modern manufacturing is bright, precise, and increasingly beautiful — not dark satanic mills. The factory as a place where craft and intelligence meet
- **Light:** High-intensity LED arrays mixed with generous natural light from high factory windows and skylights. This is the key shift: modern factories are bright environments. Safety amber on equipment as accent. Warm afternoon sun through clerestory windows creating dramatic shafts of light
- **Shot types:** World Shots for factory floors from end-to-end. People Shots for operators alongside equipment. Systems Shots for manufacturing cells and monitoring stations. Detail Shots for sensor arrays and precision instruments

### Finance & Governance
- **The world:** Modern fintech operations with glass-walled monitoring floors and city views. Co-location facilities with orderly fiber infrastructure. Bright quantitative research environments with natural light and multiple displays. Compliance operations in contemporary office settings. The infrastructure of modern finance is silicon, fiber, and real-time data — but it lives in well-designed, light-filled spaces
- **Mood:** Precise, transparent, quietly confident. Financial infrastructure should feel trustworthy and modern — not shadowy or paranoid. Glass, light, and order communicate institutional reliability
- **Light:** Natural light dominant — glass-walled spaces where city light and screen glow coexist. Warm brass task lighting as accent. Bright, clean interiors that communicate transparency. Screen glow as color accent, not primary illumination
- **Shot types:** Systems Shots for monitoring floors and operations centers. Detail Shots for trading infrastructure and displays. People Shots for professionals against city-view windows. Transition Shots for the glass boundary between physical space and digital dashboards

### Transportation & Logistics
- **The world:** Container ports with gantry cranes against clear sky. Fleet staging areas in morning light. Operations centers overlooking active yards. Smart highway corridors in daylight. Rail management centers with panoramic windows over the network. Drone delivery hubs. The orchestration of global logistics made visible
- **Mood:** Precision in motion. The choreography of global trade — but seen in the optimistic light of systems that work well. Movement, connection, delivery. Things arriving where they should
- **Light:** Clear daylight on outdoor port and rail infrastructure. Golden hour on container surfaces and crane silhouettes. Warm afternoon light in fleet staging areas. Daylit operations centers with window views onto the action
- **Shot types:** World Shots for ports and rail yards in broad light. Vantage Shots for infrastructure from above — the orchestration view. Systems Shots for operations centers. People Shots for operators overlooking active facilities

### Water & Environment
- **The world:** Water treatment facilities with settling tanks in morning light. River monitoring stations in natural settings. Reservoir spillways with water flowing and catching light. Irrigation control structures in agricultural landscape. Watershed measurement infrastructure surrounded by green
- **Mood:** Living systems. Natural and engineered flow working together. The most inherently optimistic vertical — water and environment imagery should feel fresh, vital, and hopeful. Nature and infrastructure in harmony
- **Light:** Natural backlit — morning light through water creating sparkle and transparency. Dappled forest light on monitoring equipment. Blue sky reflected in settling tanks. Emerald and green dominant, with golden warm accents from morning/evening sun
- **Shot types:** World Shots for facilities in landscape. Transition Shots for the boundary between natural water systems and engineered infrastructure. Detail Shots for monitoring equipment in natural settings. Vantage Shots for treatment campuses from above

### Broadcast & Media
- **The world:** Virtual production stages with massive curved LED walls where physical sets merge with virtual environments. IP-based master control rooms with glass walls. Cloud playout centers. SNG satellite uplink trucks deployed at live events in daylight. 8K camera systems on well-lit production sets. The broadcast infrastructure of 2026 is IP-native, cloud-distributed, and LED-powered
- **Mood:** Creative energy. The seam between physical and virtual, captured in the bright, energized atmosphere of active production. This is where technology serves storytelling — the mood should feel exciting and generative
- **Light:** LED wall illumination mixing with tungsten production lighting creates naturally warm, dramatic interiors. Daylight through studio windows or open truck bays. Golden hour on outdoor broadcast infrastructure. The light itself is content — and it's warm
- **Shot types:** World Shots for production stages and LED volumes. Transition Shots for the physical/virtual boundary. Systems Shots for master control and playout centers. Detail Shots for camera systems and production hardware

## Challenge Images: The System Under Stress

Same industry environments, but captured in a state of degradation or failure:

- **Aging infrastructure:** Rusted equipment, cracked surfaces, outdated panels with analog gauges
- **Darkness:** Environments where lights are off, screens dark, systems powered down
- **Isolation:** Empty control rooms, unmanned stations, abandoned equipment
- **Disorder:** Cable tangles, misaligned equipment, cluttered work areas
- **Environmental stress:** Fog/haze reducing visibility, water intrusion, dust accumulation

**Grading shift:** More desaturated, higher contrast, colder. Forest green shadows go deeper toward near-black. Amber warmth is absent or dying.

## Hard Rules

1. **Show the world, not the widget.** Pull back enough to see the industry environment in context
2. **Every image tells an industry story.** Someone in that industry should recognize their world
3. **Sharp throughout.** f/2.8–f/4, not f/1.8 macro blur. Context matters — keep background readable
4. **Warm analog film.** Portra 800 character. Green shadows, amber practical light
5. **Natural/practical lighting only.** Golden hour, facility lights, instrument panels, signal lamps — no studio setups
6. **Documentary authenticity.** IBM's compositional discipline — no manufactured scenes, no obvious AI generation
7. **No typography/logos/words.** Ever
8. **No identifiable human faces.** People can appear as silhouettes, motion blur, or from behind — showing scale
9. **Optimism is mandatory.** Light, bright, forward-looking. Every image should make the viewer feel good about the world they're seeing. Dark, gloomy, or dystopian imagery is reserved exclusively for Challenge images
10. **Challenge images = same world, degraded state.** Dark, desaturated, systems failing — the ONLY place darkness dominates
11. **Every image has a shot type.** World, Systems, People, Detail, Vantage, or Transition. Know which one you're making before you write the prompt
12. **The 2x grid is law.** Every image divides into two zones with a clear boundary. Subject in one, context in the other
13. **The dither shader handles the texture.** Photos should be sharp and gorgeous — the site applies its own treatment

## Anti-Patterns

- Extreme macro of single objects (that was v6 — too isolated, too abstract)
- Generic environments (a "data center" that could be any brand's photo)
- Stock photography staging — no posed scenes, no obvious setup
- Zoomed-out aerial establishing shots (too impersonal — Vantage Shots should be 20-45°, not straight overhead)
- HDR, oversaturated, or digitally-rendered look
- Neural network / brain / AI imagery
- Text, labels, or HUD overlays baked into the image (save that for v7t tactical style)
- Pure abstraction where you can't identify the industry
- **Dark, gloomy, or dystopian mood** — no overcast gray, no windowless bunkers, no night-only exteriors, no cold institutional lighting. If an image makes you feel anxious rather than optimistic, it doesn't belong
- **Sealed interiors with no natural light** — every interior should have visible connection to the outside world (windows, skylights, open doors)
- **Rain, fog, smoke, or degraded conditions** — exclusively for Challenge images
