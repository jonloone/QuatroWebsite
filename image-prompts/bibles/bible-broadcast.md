# Broadcast & Media — Visual World Bible

## The World in 2026
Broadcast infrastructure in 2026 is distributed, cloud-native, and pixel-perfect. IP-based facilities carry 100Gbps SMPTE 2110 video streams over enterprise switches. Cloud playout centers (Dublin, Singapore, Los Angeles) can originate content for any market without physical media movement. Virtual production suites use 270-degree curved LED walls (The Volume style) showing photorealistic environments in real-time. Remote production crews work across bonded cellular networks, sending pristine video from stadiums and events back to master control via satellite uplink trucks. 8K camera systems and AI-driven automated production handle composition, focus, and switching. The human creative operator still exists, but is now supported by layers of technical infrastructure and ML-assisted decision-making.

The room is dark except for emergency lighting (red emergency lights). The broadcast must continue, but the facility is running on battery/generator power. The mood is crisis management.

## Visual Don'ts

**Avoid**: Classic "TV studio" clichés with bright, colorful set design and visible audience. Modern broadcast is technical infrastructure, not theatrical entertainment.

**Avoid**: News anchors at desks reading teleprompters (tired imagery). Focus on the production infrastructure that enables the broadcast, not the talent.

**Avoid**: Stock photos of "satellite dishes in space" or outer-space imagery. Satellites are infrastructure, not the subject; focus on uplink facilities.

**Avoid**: Old-school broadcast equipment (analog mixers, magnetic tape machines, CRT monitors). 2026 broadcast is IP-based, software-driven, and cloud-enabled.

**Avoid**: Retro "control room" clichés with dramatic overhead lighting and intense operators. Real broadcast control rooms are technically sophisticated but calm and organized.

**Avoid**: Cameras on traditional tripods in traditional studio settings. Focus on robotic precision systems, remote operations, and the technical ecosystem supporting the camera, not the camera alone.

**Avoid**: "Green screen" studio setups as the primary visual. Virtual production is about the seamless integration of real talent with LED-rendered environments, not visible green screen artifacts.

### Routing Rules

**Blog Post & Content Matching**:
- Conduct keyword matching against `topic_keywords` column: blog posts about virtual production technology match to the highest-overlap hero/capability scene.
- Multiple matches: use tier precedence to break ties.

**Tier Selection by Content Type**:
- **Hero tier** scenes: Use for overview articles, thought-leadership pieces, industry vision, "state of broadcast 2026" content. Hero scenes show integrated systems at human scale.
- **Capability tier** scenes: Use for how-it-works, technical deep-dives, feature explainers, product capability pages. Capability scenes isolate a single technical achievement in detail.
- **Challenge tier** scenes: Use for problem statements, use cases demonstrating scale/stress, competitive differentiation around resilience. Challenge scenes show the same infrastructure under pressure or failure.

**Challenge Scene Rendering Rules**:
- Challenge versions use identical camera angle and composition as their hero/capability counterparts.
- Lighting is degraded: reduce brightness by 30-50%; shift from cool task lighting to desaturated warm tones (reds, muted ambers).
- Palette shift: primary Quatro colors (Cyber Emerald, Safety Amber, Brass) are desaturated to muted rust/brown equivalents; Intelligence Navy shifts toward pure black.
- Status indicators (normally green operational) flip to red alert or amber warning throughout the scene.
- Human figures show active stress or urgency (bent posture, intense focus, troubleshooting posture).

**Multi-Scene Content**:
- Long-form pieces (e.g., "The Evolution of Remote Production") may pull 2–3 scenes across tiers: hero for overview paragraph, capability for technical architecture section, challenge for resilience/future-proofing section.
- Pair scenes visually by maintaining consistent equipment/environment across tiers (e.g., Master Control Room hero + Master Control Room Alert Cascade challenge show same space under different conditions).
